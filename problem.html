Oceans Pollutants

Ocean pollution is slowly killing our oceans and the creatures that inhabit it. When people think about ocean pollution, they typically think of garbage in the oceans. However, threats to the ocean extend much farther than simply human waste, such as plastics, discarded into the ocean. In order to begin to help preserve the oceans, you must first understand all of the things that are hurting them. When we understand what we are doing to harm the oceans, we can begin to become more conscience of the choices we make and understand how to help and prevent further damage. The ocean has become the biggest victim of plastic. At least 14 million tons of plastic end up in the ocean each year, which makes up to 80% of marine debris. Animals may confuse plastic with food and attempt to eat it, which ultimately leads to the animal suffocating and dying. Plastics such as bottles and bags can take anywhere from 20-450 years to decompose. Not only do these plastics directly harm marine life, but they also harm the ocean's enviroment as they decompose due to the chemicals they contain. If plastic pollution in the ocean continues at the rate it is currently at, by 2050 it is estimated that there will be more plastic in the ocean than fish.
Oil pollution is one of the most destructive forms of ocean contamination. Large oil spills and everyday leaks from ships release millions of gallons of oil into the ocean each year. This oil spreads quickly across the surface, forming a thick layer that blocks sunlight and reduces oxygen levels in the water. Marine animals such as birds, fish, and mammals can become coated in oil, which damages their skin, feathers, and fur, often leading to hypothermia, poisoning, or death. Oil can also sink and contaminate coral reefs and ocean floors, causing long-term damage to entire ecosystems. Even small amounts of oil pollution can have lasting effects, making it extremely difficult for affected areas to fully recover.
Runoff waste is a major source of ocean pollution that often goes unnoticed. When it rains, water flows over land and collects pollutants such as trash, chemicals, fertilizers, and oils before carrying them into rivers and eventually the ocean. This polluted runoff can introduce harmful substances into marine environments, disrupting ecosystems and harming wildlife. Excess nutrients from fertilizers can cause algal blooms, which reduce oxygen levels in the water and create “dead zones” where marine life cannot survive. Runoff pollution not only affects ocean health but also impacts coastal communities that rely on clean water for fishing and recreation.
Discarded or lost fishing gear, often called “ghost gear,” is a serious threat to ocean life. Items such as fishing nets, lines, and traps can remain in the ocean for decades, continuing to trap and kill marine animals long after they are abandoned. Fish, sea turtles, and even large mammals like whales can become entangled in these materials, leading to injury, suffocation, or starvation. Because most fishing gear is made of durable plastics, it does not easily break down and can continue harming ecosystems for many years. Ghost gear not only affects marine life but also damages coral reefs and disrupts natural habitats.
Light pollution is an often overlooked but harmful form of ocean pollution. Artificial lights from coastal cities, ships, and offshore structures can disrupt the natural behaviors of marine animals. Many species rely on natural light patterns from the moon and stars to navigate, hunt, and reproduce. For example, sea turtle hatchlings use the light of the moon to find their way to the ocean, but artificial lights can confuse them and lead them away from the water. Light pollution can also interfere with feeding patterns and migration routes of fish and other marine organisms. Over time, these disruptions can negatively impact entire ecosystems.
Pesticide pollution enters the ocean through agricultural runoff and can have severe effects on marine life. Chemicals used to protect crops are often washed into nearby waterways during rainfall, eventually reaching the ocean. These substances can be toxic to fish, plankton, and other organisms that form the base of the marine food chain. As pesticides accumulate in the bodies of marine animals, they can move up the food chain in a process known as bioaccumulation, affecting larger predators and even humans who consume seafood. Long-term exposure to pesticides can disrupt reproduction, weaken immune systems, and lead to population declines in many species.
